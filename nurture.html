Nurture app Case Study



Overview

What technical solution do you imagine when you come up to the word: Mindfulness? That’s the question and the challenge my group faced in a project-based course titled Interactive Design where we would - you guessed it - design and develop an interactive and responsive mobile app. So what did we create from a single word? We decided to interpret Mindfulness as one’s awareness of their attention. With this in mind we could move onto finding real issues and discovering real solutions. Everyone is distracted by technology, but it’s a problem especially prevalent among Millenials and Gen Z who have grown up most, if not all, of their lives inundated with screens. It is impossible to detach from screens because they are parts of everyday life so the mantra behind our approach is: If you can’t beat, join em.

Problem Statement

While technology has improved every conceivable aspect of human life, it tends to be very distracting. There needs to be an intuitive and accountable way for technology to help users focus and improve their productivity.

Needfinding

I conducted my observations by asking participants to study with me and telling them I would ask questions about their overall attention after studying. In reality, my observational study was noting down every time they became clearly distracted by their phone or their laptop during a span of 20 minutes.

User 1

In the 20 or so minutes that I was actively observing User 1’s phone usage, they picked up their phone and looked away from the laptop screen 12 times. The duration of the instances ranged from a couple seconds – possibly reading and clearing a notification – to almost a whole minute. It is also important to note that I was not looking at their laptop screen so there could have been a higher number of distractions via her laptop. The main issue with User 1’s study session was the fact that they laid out their phone next to the work. This meant that every sound, vibration, and/or activation from the phone could and would distract them.

User 2

In the 20 minutes actively observing User 2’s phone usage, they picked up their phone a total of 3 times. This surprisingly lower value was due to their choice to put the phone with the screen facing down to prevent any activity from taking their attention. Additionally, they studied with AirPods so this likely deterred any external distractions from reaching them. This time, however, I was able to look at the user’s laptop screen. For privacy reasons, I didn’t note what they were looking at but instead noted that they seemed to be replying to texts and I know this from asking him afterwards. The user seemed to do this 5 times. The main issue in the session is the computer’s nature of simplifying immediate access to any information at any time. This is by no means a drawback of the computer as one could argue that it is a key benefit, but it hinders its usability and effectiveness as a tool for studying.

Storyboard

The idea presented by the storyboard is that an application would encourage a single user or a group of users to stay away from their mobile devices (arguably the most detrimental devices to our productivity). But we wanted to generalize our app to be used in any activity that could benefit from ignoring mobile devices.

We wanted to tackle this problem by giving people a visual representation that symbolizes a person’s focus and growth in self-control. Our app, named Nurture, is meant to keep users accountable while they focus on their task at hand. The app allows users to grow plants the longer they focus and browse their Garden of plants, which represents the user’s achievements and allows them to track their growth over time.

User Testing

For our user tests, we selected three college students because they are good representatives of the target audience and user base we want our app to appeal to. Since our app aims to help improve mindfulness and focus of users, college students were a good choice because they are notoriously known to get off task and become distracted.

General Takeaways
  1. In the garden page, all users clicked the image of the plant and the description intuitively. We think they did that because they wanted to see if there was anything more to the garden page. We also think that since these testers had never seen the app before, they are exploring the features of the app by clicking around to learn about and understand the app’s functionalities.
  2. Two of Three testers clicked on the hourglass after setting the timer to 15 minutes. We think that was because when they first clicked on the hourglass, a pop-up told them to first set the time. They might have thought that pop-up was telling the user to set the time first before clicking on the hourglass. Essentially, the testers assumed there was more to the functionality of the hourglass than just as a way to denote to the users how to set the time. Perhaps if the hourglass did not exist, users would not even get initially confused on how to set the timer.
  3. Two of three testers moved closer to the screen when typing out login credentials while logging in. This may be because the font size was too small or the font-color was hard to read.

Alternate Design


Page A (Left) and Page B (Right)

The next part of the process was to digest what initial user tests were providing and create multiple iterations of improvements. There. Was. Clash. My group and I could not decide what improvements were better and/or if they were necessary at all. The natural solution was letting our audience decide.

A/B testing using Google Analytics was our key to analyzing which page yielded better results. In any study, one must have an operational and measurable metric. We chose to measure visit duration. In our minds, users would stick around longer while exploring the pages that interested and captivated their attention more. For our recruitment process, we selected over 20+ college students because they are good representatives of the target audience and user base we want our app to appeal to. Since our app aims to help improve mindfulness and focus of users, college students were a good choice because, again, they are notoriously known to get off task and become distracted.

Observations for Visit Duration on Page B (Variant 1)

Median time: 3m 22s

Middle 50% of observations: 2m 18s - 5m 04s



The results suggest that users spent more time on the Page B variant of our garden. We speculate this is the case because the information was displayed in a manner that was easier for the user to browse the plants they have grown in the My Garden page and the information associated all at once compared to our Page A variant which was interactive and required the user to press on each individual plant to display more detailed information. In addition the visual design of Page B may have been more appealing than Page A, leading to users spending increased time on Page B on average.

General Thoughts on Online Feedback

From our online testing we received primarily informal feedback after our users test our app online. By and large we received positive responses to our app. Many people said that the app was cool and cute. The best response we received was a user told us the app flows seamlessly, signifying we’re doing a good job improving our user flow and app design. However, our whimsical design does have “drawbacks”, many users commented how they initially thought our app was a game instead of an app that helps to focus. To combat this, in the future our team is hoping to implement a pop-up notification detailing the purpose of the app and how the user should use the app, this information will also be included in our help page that is currently in development.

Final Note

Although this project was conducted in an academic setting, the professor and the teaching assistants provided minimal guidance to allow my team and I to feel like independent app developers in charge of the planning, designing, developing, and testing. In terms of technical skills, I got a peek at the plethora of back-end and front-end technologies often used in industry and in real-world applications. The project made me excited about server-side programming, WebSockets, and other web-related tools. In terms of designing skills, this project emphasized the importance of iterative sketches and prototypes throughout the development. There were times when I started programming without a visual prototype. These sessions took much longer and resulted in less-than-pretty interfaces. As time went on, I developed a habit of creating prototypes using Adobe XD and Figma before ever starting the code. These sessions were objectively faster and more effective. On a more personal level, this job gave me a preview of the granularities of working in a multi-faceted team where everyone has different skills and limitations. I learned to manage expectations and learned to discuss conflicting ideas or approaches.
